# Metab Taxonomy

Four categories, each a dimension of self-knowledge. Every concept gets a breadcrumb of 2-3 levels: `Category > Branch` or `Category > Branch > Sub-branch`. Branches with scientific grounding name their source. Branches without one are editorial and presented in the UI as category paths, not as scientific lineage.

## 1. Thinking (`thinking`)

How your mind reasons, judges, and misjudges. The object of awareness is your own reasoning.

| Branch | Sub-branches | Examples | Grounding |
|---|---|---|---|
| Mental Models | Reframing, Scope & Limits, Decision Tools | Inversion, First Principles, Map vs. Territory, Circle of Competence, Second-Order Thinking | Editorial. Only models about one's own cognition qualify (see Rule 5). |
| Cognitive Biases | Belief & Confirmation, Judgment & Estimation, Memory, Risk & Loss | Confirmation Bias, Anchoring, Availability Heuristic, Sunk Cost Fallacy, Rosy Retrospection | Heuristics-and-biases research (Kahneman & Tversky tradition) |
| Cognitive Distortions | - | Catastrophizing, Black-and-White Thinking, Mind Reading, Overgeneralization | CBT literature (Beck, Burns) |
| Mindsets & Self-Beliefs | - | Growth Mindset, Fixed Mindset, Learned Helplessness, Self-Efficacy, Impostor Phenomenon | Psychology of self-beliefs (Dweck, Bandura, Seligman) |

Example breadcrumbs:
`Thinking > Cognitive Biases > Belief & Confirmation` (Confirmation Bias)
`Thinking > Mental Models > Reframing` (Inversion)
`Thinking > Mindsets & Self-Beliefs` (Growth Mindset)

## 2. Feeling (`feeling`)

What you feel and how feelings work. The object of awareness is your own emotional state.

| Branch | Sub-branches | Examples | Grounding |
|---|---|---|---|
| Emotion Families | Joy, Sadness, Anger, Fear, Disgust, Surprise, Trust, Anticipation, Self-Conscious | Disappointment (Sadness), Anxiety (Fear), Contentment (Joy), Shame (Self-Conscious) | Plutchik's wheel for the family structure; Berkeley 27-emotion set as inclusion checklist. Self-Conscious added editorially per self-conscious emotion research (Tracy & Robins, Tangney) because shame, guilt, embarrassment, and pride have no Plutchik home |
| Emotional Dynamics & Skills | - | Emotional Granularity, Affect Labeling, Reappraisal, Mood vs. Emotion, Emotional Suppression | Affective science (Barrett, Gross) |

Example breadcrumbs:
`Feeling > Emotion Families > Sadness` (Disappointment)
`Feeling > Emotional Dynamics & Skills` (Emotional Granularity)

## 3. Relating (`relating`)

How you read and misread other people. The object of awareness is your perception of others.

| Branch | Sub-branches | Examples | Grounding |
|---|---|---|---|
| Empathy & Perspective-Taking | - | Cognitive Empathy, Affective Empathy, Compassion, Theory of Mind | Social cognition research |
| Social Biases & Attribution | - | Fundamental Attribution Error, Halo Effect, In-Group Bias, Projection, Spotlight Effect | Social psychology |
| Social Dynamics | - | Reciprocity, Social Proof, Sonder, Pluralistic Ignorance | Social psychology; editorial selection |

Example breadcrumbs:
`Relating > Empathy & Perspective-Taking` (Cognitive Empathy)
`Relating > Social Biases & Attribution` (Fundamental Attribution Error)

## 4. Communicating (`communicating`)

What happens when thoughts become words between people. The object of awareness is the exchange.

| Branch | Sub-branches | Examples | Grounding |
|---|---|---|---|
| Logical Fallacies | Relevance, Presumption, Ambiguity | Strawman (Relevance), Ad Hominem (Relevance), False Dilemma (Presumption), Equivocation (Ambiguity) | Standard fallacy classification from logic textbooks |
| Rhetorical Techniques | - | Ethos/Pathos/Logos, Framing, Loaded Question, Gish Gallop | Classical rhetoric; argumentation theory |
| Persuasion & Influence | - | Foot-in-the-Door, Scarcity, Authority Bias (as used on you), Dark Patterns | Persuasion research (Cialdini tradition) |
| Constructive Communication | - | Steelmanning, Active Listening, I-Statements, Nonviolent Communication | Communication practice; editorial |

Example breadcrumbs:
`Communicating > Logical Fallacies > Relevance` (Strawman Argument)
`Communicating > Constructive Communication` (Steelmanning)

## Categorization rules

Apply in order. Rule 1 decides most cases.

1. **Object of awareness.** When a user recognizes this concept in real life, what are they observing? Their own reasoning: Thinking. Their own emotional state: Feeling. Their perception of another person: Relating. An exchange of words: Communicating.
2. **Bias vs. fallacy.** Does it happen silently inside one head (bias, Thinking) or between people in language (fallacy, Communicating)? Confirmation bias needs no conversation. A strawman needs one.
3. **Socially triggered feelings stay in Feeling.** Envy, embarrassment, and jealousy are triggered by others but observed in yourself. Empathy goes to Relating because its object is the other person's state, not your own.
4. **Dual fits get one home.** Choose by the primary object of awareness, list the second reading under `related`. Example: Halo Effect is a judgment error (Thinking) about people (Relating); its primary object is your perception of a person, so Relating.
5. **The Tuesday test.** Every concept must support a 1-2 sentence scenario that could happen to a normal person on a normal Tuesday. Concepts that fail (Power Law Distribution, Pareto Principle as statistics) are out of scope regardless of fame. This test is the product boundary.

## Breadcrumb conventions

- 2 levels minimum, 3 maximum. Broad to specific.
- Level 1 is always the category display name (Thinking, Feeling, Relating, Communicating).
- Sub-branches are optional and only exist where a branch has a stable, recognized internal structure (emotion families, fallacy classes, bias clusters).
- Breadcrumbs are category paths, not ontology claims. When science offers a structure we use it; otherwise the path is editorial.
